# V5-Derived Quality Standard

## Core Standard

The article must help a buyer choose, calculate, compare, or prepare a useful project brief. It should make uncertainty easier to navigate instead of presenting several numbers as equally valid.

## Direct Answer

- Answer the main query within the first 120 words.
- Name one default recommendation when the evidence supports it.
- State who the default is for and what assumptions it uses.
- Separate a daily or normal recommendation from compact, occasional, maximum, or exceptional values.

Use this pattern when multiple ranges exist:

1. **Default:** the value most readers should select.
2. **Verified compact option:** use only after checking named constraints.
3. **Occasional maximum:** do not present as the everyday specification.
4. **Project exception:** defer to actual products, plans, standards, or qualified professionals.

## Units and Numbers

- Use one unit system throughout the article and tables.
- Default to inches for a United States or unspecified Shopify audience.
- Do not mix feet and inches in the same guide unless the user explicitly needs both; if both are required, make one primary and show conversions consistently.
- Recalculate every worked example after changing a recommendation.
- Check that capacity charts agree with the prose and FAQ.
- Label planning ranges as guidance rather than universal rules or code requirements.

## Structure

- One H1.
- Six to ten H2 sections.
- A direct-answer opening, decision framework, practical constraints, project-preparation section, CTA, and final FAQ.
- Exactly five FAQ questions.
- FAQ is the final H2; no conclusion, CTA, sources heading, or other content follows it.
- The fifth FAQ answer contains the source links so research remains at the end without breaking the FAQ-last rule.

## Intent-Matched Structure and Opening

Choose the article architecture before writing. Use the family that matches the reader's job; do not force every topic into a buying-guide sequence.

| Structure family | Use for | Typical progression |
|---|---|---|
| `case-process-analysis` | build cases and design studies | brief -> material reading -> pivotal decisions -> execution -> transferable lessons |
| `diagnostic-troubleshooting` | defects and failure questions | symptom location -> pattern classification -> cause tree -> evidence checks -> prevention or escalation |
| `decision-comparison` | versus and buying choices | decision criteria -> meaningful differences -> scenario fit -> ownership tradeoffs -> recommendation |
| `sizing-planning` | dimensions, seating, and room fit | planning default -> inputs -> calculation -> constraints -> worked scenarios -> brief |
| `material-finish` | wood, resin, color, edge, and sheen | desired outcome -> material behavior -> tradeoffs -> sample approval -> care implications |
| `delivery-installation` | access, assembly, and site readiness | route audit -> site constraints -> sequencing -> acceptance checks -> contingency |
| `care-maintenance` | cleaning and long-term ownership | surface condition -> safe routine -> avoid list -> periodic checks -> escalation |

These progressions are decision maps, not mandatory H2 wording. Vary section count, order, evidence format, and section names when the topic requires it. A case article may use chronology; a diagnostic article should organize around observable evidence; a comparison should organize around decision criteria rather than two mirrored product descriptions.

Choose one opening mode:

- `verdict-first`: give the recommendation and its assumptions.
- `constraint-first`: begin with the room, material, installation, or use constraint that changes the answer.
- `diagnostic-observation`: begin with what the reader can see, feel, locate, or measure.
- `comparison-tension`: begin with the tradeoff that makes two options genuinely different.
- `misconception-correction`: correct a specific belief, then explain the practical consequence.
- `process-stakes`: begin with the decision in the workflow that controls later outcomes.

Never use “If you want the short answer,” “The short answer is,” or a cosmetic paraphrase as a reusable first sentence. Compare the normalized first 12 words with drafts in the current batch and recent local output. Also compare H2 order and CTA trigger-plus-presentation. When similarity is high, change at least two of these three dimensions: opening mode, structure progression, CTA presentation.

## Evidence

- Use at least three credible sources for measurable or technical topics.
- Include at least one primary or authoritative source when available.
- Explain what each source supports in a short phrase.
- Make editorial recommendations distinguishable from quoted standards.
- Never convert competitor opinions into universal facts.

## Originality and Tone

- Do not copy headings, FAQ sets, sentences, examples, case studies, or CTA language from reference blogs.
- Avoid generic filler, fake quotations, invented stories, and unsupported superlatives.
- Prefer concrete verbs, buyer questions, formulas, checks, and tradeoffs.
- Keep the brand present where expertise changes the recommendation, not in every section.
- Use short paragraphs, scannable lists, and tables only when comparison benefits.

## Intent-Triggered CTA Selection

Choose exactly one primary CTA trigger and one CTA presentation before drafting the closing section. The trigger determines the job; the presentation determines the shape. Resolve trigger overlaps in this order: defect or safety, delivery or installation, room sizing, material or process, design case, buying comparison, then brand proof.

| Primary trigger | Required CTA function | Requested inputs or next step | Quantified brand proof |
|---|---|---|---|
| Design case or build analysis | State the transferable case lesson, then invite a topic-specific design brief | slab references, room dimensions, seating, resin and finish direction | off by default |
| Defect or troubleshooting | Request diagnostic evidence without promising repair | wide and raking-light photos, location, age, timing, change over time | off; never use as the diagnostic conclusion |
| Size, seating, or room fit | Convert the planning framework into a project brief | dimensioned plan, chair width, headcount, circulation, base and delivery constraints | one verified fact optional only if unused elsewhere |
| Material, color, clarity, edge, or sheen | Ask for sample or material approval | wood species/photos, resin color or clarity, sheen, room light | off by default |
| Product comparison or buying guide | Narrow the choice and link to the most relevant collection or custom brief | use case, dimensions, maintenance tolerance, candidate configuration | one fact only when supplier trust is a core objection |
| Delivery or installation | Trigger a site-access check | doors, turns, stairs, elevator, floor, unloading and installation limits | off by default |
| Care or maintenance | Point to the care guide, product instructions, or maker support | stain type, cleaner, surface condition, product instructions | off |
| Brand or supplier trust | Answer a specific credibility question, then request project information | capability concern and project brief | one verified metric; two require an explicit editorial reason |

Select the presentation by these rules:

- `evidence-or-brief-request`: use when diagnosis, sizing, delivery, or material approval cannot continue without reader evidence.
- `split-action-block`: use a neutral Conclusion/Summary followed by one short action H2 when a real, topic-relevant collection, Custom Table Journey, available-slabs page, care guide, or contact destination exists.
- `expertise-bridge`: use a pre-conclusion professional-team section when process complexity or risk control is central; name the specific risks without promising perfection.
- `summary-brand-bridge`: integrate one topic-specific brand-capability sentence into the Summary/Conclusion when the brand method is itself part of the answer. An imperative is optional.
- `integrated-conclusion`: use a soft final-sentence invitation for cases, trends, inspiration, and material-aesthetic topics after the editorial conclusion is complete.

Do not require a submission checklist in every CTA. A relevant conclusion, a single valid internal destination, or a topic-specific brand capability can be the next step. If FAQ exists, keep every CTA presentation before it and retain FAQ as the final module. Rotate presentations across adjacent drafts; do not repeat the same trigger-plus-presentation combination unless the intent genuinely requires it.

Do not automatically combine “more than 10 years” and “over 5,000 customers served.” Use the 10-year fact only for a design or manufacturing-experience objection. Use the 5,000-customer fact only for a business-maturity or service-experience objection, preserving the qualification “across the furniture business.” Most ordinary case, defect, material, care, and delivery articles should use no quantified brand proof in the CTA.

Before approval, compare the CTA against the current batch and recent drafts. Reject the draft if it repeats the same opener, the same proof combination, or the `LuxCraftWood can use...` sentence frame with only nouns changed. Every CTA must provide at least one topic-specific input, next step, valid destination, or established brand capability.

## Bilingual Parity

The Chinese review copy must preserve:

- heading order;
- numerical values and units;
- recommendation hierarchy;
- factual qualifications;
- links and source descriptions;
- CTA meaning;
- image placements and alt-text intent.

Treat a mismatch between languages as a factual error.

## Word Deliverables

- Always produce separate English and Chinese DOCX files from the approved Markdown sources.
- Treat DOCX as the primary human-facing result; supporting formats must not replace it.
- Preserve bilingual parity across headings, facts, tables, CTA, FAQ, links, and source notes.
- Use real Word structure and readable editorial styling instead of plain-text paragraphs with manual markers.
- Match the approved V4 editorial-preview structure in `docx-format-standard.md`, including first-page SEO metadata, embedded images, Calibri 10.5-point (五号) body text, no page header/footer, one-inch Letter margins, and localized Chinese labels.
- Visually inspect rendered pages when rendering is available. Missing, corrupt, clipped, or materially incomplete Word files block delivery.

## Images

Generate at least five original AI design visualizations and preferably seven for a long guide:

1. hero showing the full product or use case;
2. scale, dimensions, or seating context;
3. detail that supports a key decision;
4. working or real-use configuration;
5. design or material comparison;
6. installation, support, or access context when relevant;
7. closing image aligned with the CTA.

An image plan without finished local assets is incomplete. Every selected image must be visually reviewed, center-cropped without stretching, saved as a 1200 x 500 PNG or JPEG inside the output bundle, referenced by both languages, and embedded in both DOCX files. Every metadata item requires a purpose, insertion point, filename, local path, alt text, aspect ratio, full prompt, original-AI source type, and generated status.

Reject placeholders, duplicated assets, stock substitutions, logos, watermarks, text artifacts, malformed products, impossible geometry, or unsupported visual claims. Generated images are illustrative design concepts and must not be described as customer projects, completed products, technical drawings, certifications, or performance evidence.
